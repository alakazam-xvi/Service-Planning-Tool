About the Simulator

Overview

This simulator provides a deterministic model for transit planning. It visualizes how a fleet handles a schedule across varying operating periods, discretizing travel time changes between peak and off-peak conditions.

Route Types

  • Loop Route: Each unit departs from a single terminal and completes a full circuit back. Roundtrip Time (RT) covers the entire loop. Layover Time is terminal recovery time added on top.
  • Non-Loop Route (A ↔ B): The route has two terminals. All units are dispatched initially from Terminal A. Terminal B's first departure aligns with the moment the first unit is ready to depart from B after queuing. Separate travel times for A→B and B→A can be defined per period, supporting asymmetric traffic conditions.

Scheduling Methods

  • Uniform Trips per Vehicle: Each unit first runs independently for the full operating window. Results are then trimmed to the minimum trip count across all units, so every vehicle operates the same number of trips. Overrun trips (departures within the operating window that arrive after end of operation) are still flagged on the timetable.
  • Scheduling based on Turn Around Time: A unit is assigned a trip only if both its departure and its arrival fall within the operating window. No trip is started if the unit cannot complete it before end of operation. Results in fewer total trips compared to Uniform mode, and may leave some units idle toward the end of the operating period.

Processes & Formulas

  • Dispatch Interval (Headway):
    • Loop: (RT + Layover Time) / N
    • Non-Loop: (ttAB + Layover Time B + ttBA + Layover Time A) / N
    Period 1 cycle time is used to compute the initial vehicle stagger. The summary table shows the implied (planned) headway for each period.
  • Planned Interval vs. Realized Avg Headway: Planned Interval is the formula-based result assuming perfectly uniform vehicle spacing. Realized Avg Headway is the mean gap between consecutive actual departures, measured directly from simulation output. These diverge when cycle times differ across periods, which is the typical case.
  • Min Fleet (Target): The minimum number of vehicles needed to achieve the period's target headway, computed as ⌈Cycle Time / Target Headway⌉. If this exceeds the deployed fleet, the Service Status column flags the shortfall.
  • Overrun Trips: A trip is classified as an overrun when its departure falls within the operating window but its scheduled arrival is after the end of operation. In Uniform Trips mode, overrun trips are expected and intentional — they represent the last trip of units still in service at the close of the operating day. Overrun trips are shown as dashed bars on the Vehicle Timetable and counted in the Overrun Trips metric.
  • Peak PPHPD (Service Capacity): The theoretical maximum number of passengers the service can carry per hour per direction, computed as (60 / Peak Realized Headway) × Seating Capacity. The peak period is identified as the one with the lowest realized headway. This reflects service supply only — actual ridership is not modeled.
  • Non-Loop Offset: Terminal B's first dispatch = Start of Operation + ttAB (Period 1) + Layover Time B. Subsequent dispatches from both terminals are spaced by the same headway.
  • Trip Assignment: For any departure time, the active period determines the travel time used for the entire leg. Trips spanning a period boundary use the departure period's travel time only.

Key Assumptions

  • Homogeneous Fleet: All units are identical and operate at the assumed speeds of their respective periods.
  • Single Crew per Vehicle: No crew rotation modeled. One driver per unit for the full day.
  • Punctuality: Vehicles depart exactly as scheduled. No stochastic delays or bunching.

Limitations

  • Stochasticity: Real-world variance (boarding delays, traffic lights) leading to bus bunching is not modeled.
  • Crew Scheduling: Driver shifts, union constraints, and meal breaks are not mapped against vehicle blocks.
  • Cross-Period Legs: A leg's travel time is determined solely by the period active at departure. No interpolation across period transitions.
  • No Demand Modeling: Passenger load and ridership are not modeled. Peak PPHPD reflects theoretical service capacity, not actual passenger volume.
